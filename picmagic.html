PicMagic

PicMagic is a mobile app I worked on while I was at Cardinal Blue Software. We made PicMagic to make collaging even more fun and automated. PicMagic eliminates the content browsing and photo editing process from the collage making task flow, and users see random masks and filters applied to their photos. We launched the app and it is currently available for free on the iOS App Store.

Team

Tyler Barth (Developer), Nana Chiang (PM)

Role

UX, UI, branding

Process

User flow, wireframe, engineering, visual design, usability

Tools

Sketch, Origami, Photoshop, Illustrator

Too Many Clicks

When I joined this project, the team had a working prototype developed by our engineer. However, the original design took too many steps for users to see the fun effect on their own photos. The app starts with a dummy photo, and you click on the gallery icon to choose a photo from your library, and another click if you want to take a new photo.

Challenge

I was tasked with coming up with a new user flow that decreases the time to discover what the app does and give users a call-to-action to use their own photos.

Camera-First Solution

I sketched a new flow and wireframe where the app opens to front-facing camera mode so that the user can take their own photo faster. They are prompted to "Take a selfie" to see what the app does. The new flow teaches the user how to use the app by immediately seeing the effect on their own photos.

New Wireframe

Users open the app and see a front-facing camera prompting them to take a selfie. Once they take a photo, they'll see masks appear on their faces and a frame around the photo. They can try different themes by swiping the screen.

Saving or sharing the photo is intuitive and easy.

User Test Insights

We tested PicMagic during our regular weekly usability tests for our PicCollage app.

on-boarding

While users were able to follow the instructions with the dummy photo, it took them longer to realize what the app does. The camera-first solution let users discover the app more quickly and intuitively. Not having a dummy photo also means returning users can access the camera more quickly.

control vs. automation

Users enjoyed the surprise of seeing random masks on their faces, but wanted to adjust them afterwards. Even though the element of randomness was great, users still wish to have some control over what they are making. Some balance between automation and sense of control is required.

What I learned

Designing the user flow for this app taught me about creating a visible mental model for the user. I had to pay close attention to how a user knows "where" they are within the app and create a navigation system that makes logical sense according to a real physical space.

Further Development

I would have liked to have seen the ability to adjust masks be implemented into the app. It would have been interesting to see the app metrics over time, and see whether users access their camera or photo library more to use the app.
